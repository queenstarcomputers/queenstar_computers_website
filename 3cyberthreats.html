Major Information Security Threats and Control Measures

Queenstar Computers: 15 Dec, 2023 | Category>> Cybersecurity

In the current world, Information Security threats are a common phenomenon and are manifested in a variety of forms such as software attacks, identity theft, information extortion, theft of intellectual property, theft of information or equipment, and sabotage, among others.

Some of the major information threats are as follows:

1) Unsecure or Poorly Secured Systems

The speed and technological development often result to compromises in security measures. In some instances, systems are developed without considering security, and remain in operation in an organization as legacy systems. It is the responsibility of an organizations to identify poorly secured systems, and mitigate the threat by securing, patching, decommissioning or isolating them.

2) Social Media Attacks

Nowadays, it has become common for numerous people to use social media to share a lot of information about themselves. Attackers has capitalized on this loophole by launching attacks directly via social media, for instance by spreading malware via social media messages. Also, attackers are indirectly, using information obtained from social media sites to analyse user and organizational vulnerabilities, and use them to design an attack.

3) Social Engineering

This entails attackers sending emails and messages that trick users into performing actions that may compromise their security or reveal private information. In this regard, attackers manipulate users using psychological triggers such as curiosity, urgency or fear. Since the source of a social engineering message seems to be true, people have a high probability of complying. For instance, a user can be prompted to click a link that installs malware on their device, or provide personal information, credentials, or financial details. Organizations can mitigate social engineering by creating awareness among users (employees) so that they are aware of its dangers. Also, training them to identify and avoid suspected social engineering messages. In addition, technological systems can be used to block social engineering at its source, or prevent users from performing dangerous actions such as clicking on unknown links or downloading unknown attachments.

4)Security Misconfiguration

Most organizations nowadays use a huge number of technological platforms and tools, such as web applications, databases, and Software as a Service (SaaS) applications, or Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS) from providers such as Amazon Web Services. Enterprise grade platforms and cloud services have security features, but these must be configured by the organization. Security misconfiguration as a result of negligence or human error can result in a security breach. There is also the problem of problem is “configuration drift”, where correct security configuration become outdated without the knowledge of the organisations ICT personnel thus exposing the system to vulnerability. Organizations can mitigate security misconfiguration using technological platforms that continuously monitor systems, identify configuration gaps, and alert or even automatically remediate configuration issues that make systems vulnerable.

5) Lack of Encryption

Encryption encode data so that it can only be decoded by users with secret keys. It is very effective in preventing data loss or corruption in case of equipment loss or theft, or in case organizational systems are compromised by attackers. Unfortunately, organizations often overlook this due to its complexity and lack of legal obligations associated with proper implementation. However, an organization can play safe by adopting encryption through purchasing of secured storage devices, using cloud services that support encryption, and using dedicated security tools.

6) Malware on Endpoints

Organizational users work with a large variety of endpoint devices, including desktop computers, laptops, tablets, and mobile phones, most of which are privately owned and not under the organization’s control, and all of which connect regularly to the Internet. A primary threat on all these endpoints is malware, which can be transmitted by a variety of means, can result in compromise of the endpoint itself. An organization can secure its systems against malware on endpoints by securing them endpoint detection and response (EDR) technology.
